Design has been a big part of my life for some time now. It's funny that you start creative design with the lofty ideas of building worlds and universes for others to exist within. There is a touch of megalomania in all designers. Tolkien could have just told a story; simply written down the adventures of Frodo and Sam beyond the Shire, but he went farther in designing his world. He provided a visual reference for his world. He designed communities, languages, and religions to inhabit his world. That vision is the difference between just telling a story and creating an experience.

User Experience (UX) is just as important as the User Interface (UI) that guides it. If users can't feel comfortable with how to navigate the information of a website, it doesn't matter how "cool" it looks. People will go elsewhere. The site needs to make sense within it's respective context. Tolkien including a map of Middle Earth in his books allowed for a more interactive involvement to his story. It provided boundaries to peoples' imaginations that they could follow while still allowing them to fill in the appropriate gaps.

UX/UI Design can often be an arduous process. Balancing the needs of the user against the wants of the client is typically a delicate tight-rope act. The biggest question is always; "How do I begin?" Find your client needs and expectations: Who is their target audience? What are they looking to accomplish? What is the story they are trying to tell? When are they wanting to have their project ready? Does the client have any examples of design that they have liked... why?

About Me

So how did I get here? I started with a pipe dream of art and animation at the beginning of my higher education. I've always had a drive for the creative. I soon discovered that web development was a growing technology that provided near immediate results. The problem, however, was that there was no guided means to learn the ins and outs of the fertile land of the internet.

We were taught the psychology of color and form, the nuances of how shape and light make you feel. No one had a clue how to adequately teach what it meant to plan an interactive user interface. Then, they were crude and rudimentary and, by and large, focused on periodical layouts and static page design. The internet grows and changes from one device to the next; from one user to the next. While the foundational ideologies of generations of print design are still very much tried and true, there is always more that digital interactive mediums bring.
